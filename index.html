Summary

I am a postdoctoral fellow, currently visiting the Bio-Computation group at Microsoft Research Cambridge, UK. This is a continuation of the work started with Alfonso Rodríguez-Patón and the LIA group in Faculty of Informatics in the Universidad Politécnica de Madrd.

I am currently focusing on synthetic biology, designing and simulating useful and interesting biological systems implemented by our collaborators at the University of Santander. I study the computing potential of these systems using computational complexity theory. That is, developing new models of computation and algorithms for these models to better understand and control biological systems. My broader interest looking at the physical world through the lens of theoretical computer science.

Here is my first plasmid.

My PhD thesis was on identifying what classes of problems can be tackled by an abstract mathematical model of cellular division. My special focus was on problems that could be solved using a feasible amount of time and very little memory (molecules). "Uniformity conditions for membrane systems: Uncovering complexity below P" (pdf), and is from the Computer Science Department of NUI Maynooth. My supervisors were Damien Woods and Tom Naughton. I defended my thesis in March 2010.

The P-Conjecture and the computational power of cellular division.

Explanation

Cell division seems to allow cellular computers to create an exponential number of self-replicating parallel computers which would allow us to solve NP complete problems in a feasible amount of time, thus revolutionising the world of computation. However, brute force approaches, where every possible solution is generated and tested are clearly a non-starer. Current algorithms can solve problems of size 125 without much effort. However, a brute force cellular computer implemented using e. coli working on the same problem would require a mass of bacteria equal to the moon, of which perhaps only 1 bacterium has the correct answer!

So perhaps with a cleverer strategy (similar to those in software) cellular computers can solve NP complete problems in polynomial time? In 2007 it was conjectured that without some way of a cell to signal to the outside world, that it was impossible to solve the hardest problems in NP.

The conjecture is still open, but we have made significant progress to proving it.

In [WodissNL] we showed that if the cells cannot dissolve then exponential division cannot help and the system probably cannot help you.

In [Symmetric] we showed that when the cells divide, if both are the same, that is symmetric, to borrow a term from stem cells. Then it is possible to simulate the system with an algorithm that stores the number of copies of each unique type of membrane there are in the system.

We then looked at the two key points, dissolving membranes and cell division, again we showed that despite the exponential blow up, the system can be simulated in polynomial time.

These two systems can be simulated in polynomial time so, unless P = NP, or P = NC these systems will not be useful as parallel computers.
